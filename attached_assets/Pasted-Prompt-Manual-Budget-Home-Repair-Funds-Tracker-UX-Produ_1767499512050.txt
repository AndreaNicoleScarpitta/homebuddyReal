Prompt: Manual Budget & Home Repair Funds Tracker – UX + Product Expert
Your Role
You are a senior product designer + UX strategist with experience in:
consumer finance tools,
budgeting and fund allocation,
anxiety-sensitive domains (money, home ownership),
mobile-first UX (React Native),
systems that integrate planning, tracking, and decision support.
You are designing a manual budget and funds tracker that integrates seamlessly into an existing Home Maintenance & Repair mobile app.
Your goal is to make it easy, calming, and empowering for non-expert homeowners to:
understand what home projects they can afford,
plan repairs against available funds,
update spending over time,
and reduce “money paralysis.”
Product Context (Assume True)
Users already have:
a prioritized home maintenance plan (Now / Soon / Later / Monitor),
cost ranges per task,
DIY vs Pro guidance,
contractor discovery (optional).
The app is not a bank or accounting system.
This is a manual tracker:
users input numbers themselves,
no automatic bank sync in MVP.
Users may have:
a dedicated “home repair fund,”
multiple informal buckets (savings, emergency fund, monthly cash),
emotional resistance to spending savings.
Core Problem to Solve
Homeowners ask:
“Can I actually afford this right now?”
And they need to answer that quickly, visually, and without spreadsheets.
Design Objectives
1. Budget Visibility (At a Glance)
Design a system where users can clearly see:
total funds allocated to home repairs,
how much is:
available,
planned,
already spent,
how upcoming projects map to those funds.
The user should be able to answer in under 10 seconds:
“What can I afford to do next?”
2. Manual Fund Setup (Low Friction)
Design the setup flow so users can:
add one or more funds (e.g. “Home Repair Fund”, “Emergency Savings”),
assign:
starting balance,
optional monthly contribution,
optional mental label (“Do not touch unless critical”).
Constraints:
No jargon (avoid “accounts,” “ledgers,” “envelopes” unless tested).
No shame or judgment.
Must feel optional, not mandatory.
3. Project-to-Budget Mapping
Design how projects interact with money:
For each repair or maintenance item:
allow users to:
earmark funds (soft allocation),
mark as “planned but not committed,”
mark as “paid” or “completed.”
show:
estimated cost range vs available funds,
confidence indicator (low / medium / high estimate certainty).
Critical UX question:
How do users see tradeoffs without feeling overwhelmed?
4. Decision Support (Not Just Tracking)
This is not a spreadsheet.
Design affordance so the system helps users reason:
“You can afford this now.”
“You may want to wait until next month.”
“Doing X first reduces risk even if Y is more visible.”
Constraints:
No financial advice claims.
No pressure language.
Use supportive, neutral phrasing.
5. Emotional UX & Money Anxiety
Home repairs trigger:
loss aversion,
fear of depletion,
regret (“I wanted to use this fund for something else”).
Design principles:
Normalize spending on prevention.
Explicitly validate hesitation.
Distinguish:
emergency spending
protective spending
optional upgrades
Example tone:
“This repair protects your home from higher costs later.”
6. Update & Maintenance Flow
Design how users update over time:
mark expenses as:
estimated,
partially paid,
fully paid.
allow easy edits (nothing is permanent).
support:
notes (“contractor quoted higher than expected”),
receipts/photos (optional).
Avoid:
heavy bookkeeping,
reconciliation workflows,
guilt-inducing “overspent” language.
7. Integration Points
Design how the budget tracker connects to:
maintenance plan (Now / Soon / Later),
contractor quotes,
push notifications (“You can now afford X”),
chat (“Can I afford this repair?”).
The assistant should be able to answer:
“Based on your current funds, here’s what’s realistic.”
